Employee Relations · Investigation Scenario 01

The Abrasive Trainer

A long-term employee is your best trainer on paper — and a recurring problem in practice. A new hire has filed a formal complaint. Here's how I'd work through it.

The situation

A long-term employee who is highly experienced and knowledgeable is responsible for training new hires. Multiple new employees have reported that she is brash, dismissive, and impatient during training. One recent trainee has formally complained, stating they feel unsupported and discouraged.

The long-term employee has a strong performance record and is seen as valuable to the business, but her behaviour is beginning to affect team morale and onboarding effectiveness.

First: two tracks, depending on where things stand

The most important thing is to address the behaviour clearly and quickly. How that looks depends on timing. If concerns have surfaced informally — through a manager or a hallway conversation — I'd bring it to the employee's direct manager to address directly, before it escalates. But once a formal complaint is filed, we're obligated to investigate. That's where we are here.

The employee who came forward

Before anything else, I'd acknowledge the person who filed the complaint. They did the right thing, their experience matters, and we'll investigate and follow up when it's done. I'd also set clear expectations upfront: they'll hear the outcome — substantiated, partially substantiated, or unsubstantiated — but any resulting actions taken with the other employee are confidential. That's a boundary worth establishing early so there's no confusion later.

Running the investigation

I'd start with the trainer's direct supervisor. Have they witnessed the behaviour themselves, or heard anything from other employees? From there, I'd speak with anyone else who may have been affected — other new hires, people who've worked alongside her during onboarding — reminding each person that the process is confidential.

The long-term employee is interviewed last. That sequencing matters: you want a complete picture before you sit down with the person at the centre of it.

Direct questions make for a stronger investigation. "Did you tell the new hire they were a slow learner?" is more useful than "Did you say anything that might have upset them?"

In the interview with the trainer, I'd start broad — walk me through a recent training shift from the beginning — then narrow toward the specific incidents. Vague questions invite vague answers. The more specific the question, the harder it is to sidestep.

The outcome conversation

In most cases, this conversation should be delivered by the trainer's manager, with HR in the coaching and preparation role. That's good practice for a few reasons: it keeps the manager in the relationship, and it builds their confidence for future difficult conversations. In practice, I'd sit in on anything particularly sensitive, or step in directly if the manager isn't ready for it yet.

Whatever the delivery, the conversation needs to cover a few things clearly:

  • The purpose of the meeting and our responsibility to address what was reported.
  • The specific behaviours and what the investigation found.
  • The impact — framed around how her actions were received, not as a character judgment. "People feel reluctant to ask you questions" lands differently than "you're intimidating."
  • Clear expectations going forward, with consequences stated plainly.
  • An explicit statement that retaliation against the person who complained won't be tolerated.
  • An offer of support — coaching, mentoring, or a conversation about what's driving the impatience. Voluntary, but genuinely available.

The framing I try to use in these conversations: we're raising this because we value her and want her to succeed here. That's not spin — it's usually true, and it changes the tone of the room.

Closing the loop with the complainant

Once the outcome conversation is done, I'd go back to the employee who came forward. Tell them their concerns were substantiated and that action has been taken, even if I can't share what that action was. Encourage them to keep working with the trainer where possible — a transfer is a last resort, not a default — but make clear that retaliation isn't tolerated and that my door stays open.

Preventing it from happening again

Proactively, the building blocks are straightforward: a clear employee handbook with respectful workplace language, and managers who know what to do when they see warning signs before a formal complaint is filed. After this incident specifically, the follow-through matters. The expectations we set in that conversation need to hold — which means checking in, documenting, and being willing to act if the behaviour continues.
