Good Intentions Don't Erase Discrimination

A BC Human Rights Tribunal decision involving a young worker with disabilities and a family-run convenience store, and what it means when "we were just trying to help" is not a defence.

Compliance & Human Rights · 5 min read

A recent decision of the BC Human Rights Tribunal, Aolick v. A1 Convenience Store and others (No. 2), highlights how discrimination can arise even when an employer believes it is doing the right thing. The case turns on two ideas that HR and frontline managers often misunderstand: that intention to discriminate is not required for a breach of the Human Rights Code, and that arrangements which begin as supported "placements" can become employment relationships — with a full duty to accommodate.1

The background

The complainant, a young woman with significant lasting effects from childhood brain cancer — including a seizure disorder and fatigue — entered the workforce through an unpaid work experience placement at a family-owned convenience store. Over time, the arrangement evolved into a wage-subsidized position through community agencies, then into a small paid role funded directly by the store at roughly six hours per week. The respondents were relatives who genuinely saw themselves as giving her a start in the labour market.1

The Tribunal found that, notwithstanding those intentions, the respondents had discriminated against her on the basis of physical and mental disability. It upheld three of her allegations: that she was not allowed to bring her trained seizure-alert service dog to work; that she experienced hurtful comments related to her disabilities; and that the termination of her employment was influenced by her disabilities.1

Intent vs. impact

A central theme in the Tribunal's reasons is the distinction between intent and impact. It accepted that the owners' son, who coordinated the arrangement, wanted to help a family member and did not set out to harm her. At the same time, the Tribunal emphasized what the Human Rights Code expressly provides: intention to discriminate is not required. What matters is whether the complainant experienced an adverse impact in employment and whether a protected characteristic — here, disability — was a factor in that impact.1,2

Good intentions are not a legal defence. The question is whether the effect of the employer's decisions disadvantaged a worker because of a protected characteristic.

The denial of the service dog removed a measure the complainant relied on to manage her seizures, creating an adverse impact directly linked to her disability. The respondents had not demonstrated that allowing the dog would cause undue hardship, nor that they had genuinely explored how to accommodate it. Similarly, while the store faced real operational constraints at the time of the termination, the Tribunal concluded that disability was a factor in the decision and that the respondents had not shown they could not have accommodated her continued employment.1,3

When a "placement" becomes employment

Another important element is how the Tribunal characterized the relationship over time. While the arrangement began as unpaid work experience and involved wage subsidies, the Tribunal treated the complainant's time at the store as employment in different phases rather than as something outside the employment relationship altogether.

The store signed agreements, supervised her work as a retail clerk, set her schedule, and — after the subsidy ended — paid her wages directly. On those facts, the Tribunal concluded she was an employee for purposes of the Code, and that the respondents therefore owed her the duty not to discriminate and to accommodate her disability to the point of undue hardship.1

This matters for any organization that hosts students, placement workers, or subsidized hires. The label on the arrangement is less important than how it actually functions. If you are directing the work, setting the schedule, and integrating someone into your operations, human rights obligations likely apply.

What this means in practice

For organizations of any size, the decision underscores a few things:

  • Arrangements that begin as unpaid placements or agency-subsidized roles may still give rise to an employment relationship for human rights purposes, particularly where the worker is integrated into regular operations and later paid directly.
  • Good intentions or a sense of "helping" do not insulate an employer from findings of discrimination if the effect of their decisions is to disadvantage a worker because of a protected characteristic.
  • Where disability is in play, decision-makers may be required to show not only that they had business reasons for their actions, but also that they meaningfully considered and, where possible, implemented accommodation before concluding that a role could not continue.1,2,3

The harder question this case raises isn't legal — it's cultural. Organizations that genuinely want to support workers with disabilities need accommodation processes that match that intention. Good intent without a real process is where most of the liability in this area lives.

Employment laws vary by province and jurisdiction. Consult a qualified employment lawyer or your HR partner for guidance specific to your situation.

  2. Human Rights Code, RSBC 1996, c. 210, s. 2 and s. 13, as interpreted in Aolick v. A1 Convenience Store and others (No. 2), 2026 BCHRT 62.
