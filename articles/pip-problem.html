Performance Management

The PIP Problem: Are We Actually Trying to Help?

Performance improvement plans have a near-zero success rate at many organizations. Before asking whether the paperwork is clean, it's worth asking whether anyone actually meant for them to work.

Search "performance improvement plan" on any career forum and the advice is almost universal: start updating your resume. One commenter on r/LifeProTips, in a post that received over 43,000 upvotes, put it plainly: employees who are placed on PIPs often do not retain their positions once the improvement period concludes, even if they genuinely strive to meet the objectives. That sentiment has become conventional wisdom — and in many organizations, may not be entirely wrong.1

What is striking is how openly some HR professionals now say this, and how little discomfort they seem to feel about it. Jessica D. Winder, an HR executive with over 110,000 LinkedIn followers, posted a video in early 2026 noting that in fourteen years working in HR, she had seen exactly one person successfully complete a PIP. Her advice to employees: treat it as a timer, not a development plan.2 What that video does not address is what role the HR function played in shaping those outcomes in the first place.

The numbers are hard to ignore

There is no peer-reviewed database tracking PIP outcomes, but practitioner evidence points in one direction. One HR professional who has supported hundreds of PIPs noted publicly that between 60% and 90% of employees on a PIP ultimately leave the organization — through resignation or involuntary separation — with only a small fraction achieving genuine long-term improvement.3 The Wall Street Journal reported in late 2024 that performance actions are on the rise, with the rate of workers subject to PIPs climbing from 33.4 per 1,000 employees in 2020 to 43.6 per 1,000 in 2023.4

At some point, the conversation has to expand from "how do we document this?" to "are we actually trying to help someone get better — and is the process set up to make that possible?"

The paper trail question

Based on evidence from both the employee and HR sides, PIPs seem to be frequently initiated not as a genuine improvement effort, but as documentation groundwork for a termination decision that has already been made.

Employment law firms across Canada have noted this directly. Osuji & Smith, a Calgary employment law firm, states that "a PIP is not an opportunity for the employer to set the stage for dismissing an employee without severance" and that an employer doing exactly that "can be liable to pay additional damages for bad faith and dishonesty." The Hum Law Firm in Toronto goes further, noting that a bad-faith PIP can justify a constructive dismissal claim, and citing a 2010 Ontario case where an employee was awarded $45,000 due to the employer's failure to carry out the PIP in good faith.5,6

In other words: if the plan was never real, it may not protect you the way you think it does.

Under Canadian law, the bar for just cause termination is high. Courts look for prior warnings, genuine opportunities to improve, a pattern of documented concerns, and progressive discipline applied consistently. A PIP issued after months of positive performance reviews — or one with goals bearing no resemblance to what was ever actually asked of the employee — does not tell a consistent story. It tells an obvious one.7

What it actually costs to give up on someone

Before reaching for a PIP-as-exit-strategy, a basic calculation is worth doing. Replacing an employee is expensive. Gallup puts the cost of replacing a frontline worker at approximately 40% of their annual salary; a technical or professional role runs about 80%; a manager or leader can reach 200% of annual salary — before accounting for the productivity loss during the transition, which typically runs six to twelve months before a new hire reaches full effectiveness.9

There is also no guarantee the replacement performs any better. A new hire arrives with a ramp-up period, cultural adjustment, and their own unknowns. An existing employee, given genuine support, brings institutional knowledge, established relationships, and a baseline of demonstrated capability. The investment in helping them succeed is usually the more rational economic choice — and for many organizations, the approach that best aligns with their stated values.

SMART goals are not enough

Most managers and HR professionals are familiar with SMART goals — Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound. As a PIP framework, SMART is helpful but not sufficient.

The achievable dimension deserves particular attention in a performance context. Goals designed to be failed are not goals — they are traps. Canadian courts have recognized this: if a PIP contains unrealistic targets, it can form the basis of a constructive dismissal claim, not defend a termination as just cause. The goals need to reflect what was actually expected of the employee in the role, not a retroactively heightened bar.

Time-bound also deserves more than a closing date. Timely feedback during the PIP — regular check-ins on schedule, progress updates delivered when promised — is a matter of basic fairness to the employee and a key ingredient in any learning process. Leaving someone on a plan for weeks with no feedback is not performance management. It is waiting.

But here is what SMART does not address at all: the manager's role in the outcome.

That is where a second S comes in. Call it SMARTS — with the final S standing for Supported.

Before a plan is issued, and throughout its duration, a key question needs to be answered in writing: what specific support will the manager and organization provide to help this employee succeed? Rather than vague commitment, this step has to be concrete and as well documented as the performance concern itself. Examples:

  • "I will schedule a 30-minute check-in each Friday to review your work and answer questions."
  • "You will be enrolled in [specific training] within the first two weeks of this plan."
  • "You will spend one day shadowing a colleague to observe how they handle [specific process]."
  • "I will connect you with a resource person or mentor who can support your development in this area."

Brightmine cites Gallup research showing that managers account for approximately 70% of the variance in employee engagement — and that positive reinforcement consistently outperforms negative pressure as a driver of improved performance.11 If a manager's influence on performance is that significant, it stands to reason that what the manager actually does during the plan period matters enormously.

From a legal standpoint, this is not just good practice — it is protection. Osuji & Smith note that if an employee "is denied the necessary resources or guidance for achieving the targets of the PIP, there might be grounds for claiming constructive dismissal." A plan that clearly documents the support offered strengthens the employer's position — while also increasing the actual probability that the employee improves.5

This is bigger than PIPs

For frontline managers: SMARTS is not just a PIP framework. It is a coaching framework. The question "what specific support will I provide?" is worth asking in any performance conversation, formal or informal. Long before a situation reaches the level of a formal plan, regular coaching conversations that include a concrete offer of help — not just a list of things to improve — tend to prevent the conditions that lead there in the first place.

For HR professionals: when was the last time your organization asked not just whether PIPs are being completed properly, but whether the underlying intent was genuine improvement or documentation for a foregone conclusion? If PIPs in your organization have a near-zero success rate, that is worth investigating — not to judge the managers who issue them, but to ask whether the process is functioning as designed.

The goal of a performance improvement process is to give a struggling employee every realistic opportunity to succeed — and to document, honestly, what both parties did during that process. When that is done well, one of two things tends to happen: performance genuinely improves, and the organization keeps a capable employee it has just invested in. Or the performance does not improve, and the record reflects a fair, supported, and well-documented process that any adjudicator would have difficulty finding fault with.

Either outcome is better than the alternative.
