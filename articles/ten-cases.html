Ten Canadian Legal Cases Every New Manager Needs to Know

There's a good chance the HR policy you're expected to enforce traces back to one of these decisions. You don't need to memorize the case names — but understanding the pattern they create changes how you manage.

Some organizations do an excellent job training managers on HR policies, and some don't — but even where training is strong, the "why" behind the rules is often missing. These ten cases are ones employment lawyers and HR people refer to consistently. They're not the only important decisions in Canadian employment law, but if you understand these, you're ahead of most managers.

This article is for general informational purposes only and is not legal advice. When in doubt, talk to an employment lawyer or your HR team.

Case 1
Re Rizzo & Rizzo Shoes Ltd., [1998] 1 SCR 27

When Rizzo & Rizzo Shoes went bankrupt, the trustee argued employees weren't owed termination pay because technically no one had been "terminated" — the bankruptcy simply ended things. The Supreme Court of Canada said no: employment standards legislation had to be read in a way that protected employees, not in a narrow, technical way.1

The case confirmed that employment standards must be interpreted broadly and in favour of employees.2 That employee-protective lens shows up again and again in employment and labour decisions. Geoffrey England's Individual Employment Law puts it plainly — most employment relationships have a power imbalance, and that imbalance is exactly what employment standards legislation exists to correct.3

What this means

Don't treat statutory minimums as loopholes to work around. Courts assume there's a power imbalance between employers and employees, and they read the law to correct it. If you're taking a creative route around basic rights, assume it will not go well.

Case 2
Brake v. PJ-M2R Restaurant Inc., 2017 ONCA 402

Esther Brake managed McDonald's franchise locations for 25 years. She was put on a performance improvement plan that the trial judge later described as designed to fail: shifting goals, moving targets, and documentation for a decision that had already been made.4 When she didn't meet the targets, she was told: accept a demotion or leave.

She left. The Court of Appeal found she had been constructively dismissed — meaning fired without calling it a firing. A significant demotion in role, status, or pay wasn't a "choice"; it was equal to a termination.5 The employer also argued she should have accepted the demotion to reduce her damages. The court rejected that — employees don't have to mitigate by working in conditions of humiliation or serious loss of status.6

What this means

Changing someone's role, pay, or reporting structure without their agreement can amount to a dismissal. Performance plans shouldn't be treated as a formality with a pre-determined conclusion. Ask yourself how a major demotion or hours cut would look if a judge read the story later.

Case 3
R v. Hritchuk, 2012 QCCS 4525

At a Quebec auto repair shop, a service manager let workers use an improvised fuel pump. Two employees were badly burned. The manager — not just the company — was charged under the Criminal Code.7

After the Westray mine disaster, Parliament added a provision that anyone who "directs the work of another person" has a personal legal duty to take reasonable steps to prevent bodily harm.8 In serious cases that can mean criminal charges for the individual manager, not just the organization. Prosecutors reserve Criminal Code charges for the most serious situations, and most safety issues will be handled under provincial occupational health and safety laws — which can also impose fines and, in some circumstances, personal liability on supervisors.9

What this means

Safety is not the job of HR or the Safety Officer alone. If you direct work, you personally own a duty to take reasonable steps to keep people safe. Follow safety procedures precisely, and if something looks unsafe, stop the work and escalate it.

Case 4
Wallace v. United Grain Growers Ltd., [1997] 3 SCR 701 & Honda Canada Inc. v. Keays, 2008 SCC 39

Jack Wallace was a top salesperson with 14 years of service who was let go badly: false allegations of cause, damaging rumours, and a process designed to humiliate. The Supreme Court extended his notice period specifically because of how the termination was handled.10 In Honda v. Keays, the Court refined how damages work — bad faith in dismissal is addressed through extended notice and, in some cases, separate damages.11

Bad faith can include alleging cause you can't support, giving pretextual reasons, spreading or tolerating damaging rumours, or publicly humiliating the person on the way out. Malicious intent isn't required — careless disregard for someone's dignity can be enough.

What this means

Having a legitimate reason to terminate doesn't protect you from how you do it. Be honest. Don't allege "cause" unless you can prove it. Don't weaponize rumours or embarrassment. Poor process can translate directly into more money owed.

Case 5
McKinley v. BC Tel, [2001] 2 SCR 161

Martin McKinley took a leave of absence from BC Tel. Upon returning, he indicated his doctor had advised him to return to a less stressful position. His employer discovered the doctor had actually cleared him to return to his original duties. BC Tel dismissed him without notice, arguing that any dishonesty of any kind was enough to amount to just cause. The Supreme Court disagreed.12

The case clarifies that "just cause" requires a full contextual analysis: the nature and seriousness of the misconduct, the employee's role, and the entire employment history. The question is whether the misconduct damages the employment relationship so badly that continuing it is impossible.13 The same act might justify termination for a new employee in a sensitive role, but not for a 20-year veteran with a strong record whose misstep is out of character. "Zero-tolerance" language in policy doesn't override that contextual approach.

What this means

Firing someone for cause — with no notice or severance — is legally hard to justify. Most situations that feel like "obvious cause" in the moment don't meet the actual standard. Involve HR early for concerns around performance or conduct.

Case 6
Potter v. New Brunswick Legal Aid Services Commission, 2015 SCC 10

David Potter was suspended indefinitely — with pay — while his employer worked on a plan to terminate him. He was given no clear explanation, just effectively sent home. The Supreme Court found constructive dismissal.14 The employer had no authority to suspend him indefinitely without a legitimate, communicated reason. Because the real motivation was being concealed, Potter could reasonably conclude his employment relationship had effectively ended.

Courts have applied similar reasoning when employers unilaterally remove duties, suspend employees, or leave them "on the bench" without clear communication and legitimate grounds.

What this means

If you're removing someone's duties or significantly changing their role, communicate clearly and act within your authority. Silence is not neutral — even if the person is still being paid, leaving them without explanation can have legal consequences.

Case 7
Gordon Food Service Canada Ltd., 2024 BCLRB 130

During a Teamsters organizing campaign at Gordon Food Service, the employer disciplined and terminated three employees. The BC Labour Relations Board found those actions were tainted by anti-union motivation.15 It ordered reinstatement, back pay, and a mandatory meeting between the union and employees on company time — with management excluded.

During a union drive, there are clear lines you cannot cross: interrogating people about union activity, threatening consequences, promising benefits if the union loses, or disciplining anyone for exercising their right to organize.17 Even the timing of discipline around union activity will be scrutinized.

What this means

If a union drive starts, call HR or legal immediately. Keep operations normal, apply policies consistently, share only factual information, and avoid anything that could look like interference. Everything you do during a campaign will be scrutinized.

Case 8
McGregor v. Morelli and Quarterway Hotel, 2006 BCHRT 277

During a job interview, Ms. McGregor was asked about her age, marital status, and whether she had children. The interview ended shortly after she answered. She was qualified. Someone else was hired. The BC Human Rights Tribunal found the employer had discriminated — not because it could prove intent, but because the questions themselves signalled that protected characteristics had entered the hiring decision.18

BC's Human Rights Code prohibits discrimination based on protected grounds in job advertisements and interviews.19 The test isn't whether the interviewer meant to discriminate. The fact that the questions were asked is enough to raise an inference that those characteristics influenced the outcome. The same rule applies across provinces.

What this means

If your organization has provided a list of approved interview questions, follow it precisely. That list exists for a legal reason. Going off-script with questions that touch on a candidate's personal life, background, or family situation — even conversationally — can expose the organization to a human rights complaint. If you have a genuine concern about availability or ability to do the job, ask directly about the schedule or physical requirements instead.

Case 9
BC Public Service Employee Relations Commission v. BCGSEU (Meiorin), [1999] 3 SCR 3

Tawney Meiorin was a forest firefighter who failed a standardized aerobic fitness test and lost her job. Evidence showed the test was harder for most women to pass and wasn't clearly necessary to perform the actual firefighting work. The Supreme Court found the standard discriminatory and built what's now known as the Meiorin test.20

Employers must design workplace standards to be inclusive where possible. A rule that adversely affects a protected group must be justified as a bona fide occupational requirement — and even then, employers must accommodate to the point of undue hardship.21 This framework sits behind much of what managers experience day-to-day: modified duties, adjusted schedules, exceptions to dress codes, flexibility around attendance for disability-related reasons, or religious observances.

What this means

Avoid one-size-fits-all rules on hours, duties, or physical standards, and don't say "that's just the job" without checking. When an employee raises medical, religious, or other human-rights-based needs, your job is to engage HR and look for reasonable accommodation — not to dismiss it as special treatment.

Case 10
Robichaud v. Canada (Treasury Board), [1987] 2 SCR 84

In Robichaud, the Supreme Court established that employers are liable for discriminatory harassment committed by their employees in the course of employment — even if management didn't directly participate.22 Doing nothing is not a defence.

That liability framework, combined with provincial occupational health and safety legislation, means employers have a legal duty to investigate harassment and to respond to incidents even when there's no formal written complaint — and even when the person affected says they "don't want to make a big deal out of it." Ontario's Divisional Court confirmed as recently as 2024 that the obligation to investigate is triggered the moment an employer is aware of a potential incident.23,24

What this means

You can't ignore harassment because it seems minor, private, or "between friends." Your role is to take concerns seriously, avoid minimizing or blaming, make sure they reach HR or the right internal process, and keep a basic record of what you observed and reported. "Just joking" is not a defence.

Bringing it back to daily management

You don't need to memorize these case names. What matters is the pattern they create: the legal floor is higher — and more employee-protective — than most managers assume. Demotions, suspensions, and jokes can have legal consequences just as much as formal terminations. Safety, accommodation, and harassment are your responsibilities too, not only HR's.

If you change roles, hours, duties, or how you treat someone on the way out, ask yourself one question: if a judge or labour board read this story later, would it look fair? If you're not sure, that's the moment to stop and call HR.
