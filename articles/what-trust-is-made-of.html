What Trust Is Made Of

The research calls it belonging. The plain word for what employees feel when they have it is trust. Three things have to be working at once for any workplace to produce either — and the instinct to fix the wrong one is what costs companies the most.

Leading Teams · 11 min read

More than twenty years on retail floors, twelve of them in management, taught me one pattern more clearly than any other. Something would go wrong on a team — a wave of resignations, a complaint that should not have escalated, a manager whose people were stalling — and the first instinct, almost always, was to ask whether the policy was clean, whether the handbook covered it, whether the documentation was tight.

Clear policy and consistent process matter more than most workplaces give them credit for, and a competent HR function makes a real difference in places where neither has existed before. Over and over again, though, the policy turned out to be the smallest part of the problem. The real failure was somewhere else, and we kept reaching for the binder anyway because the binder was the thing we knew how to fix.

The research community has spent the last decade documenting what happens when workplaces get this right and what it costs when they get it wrong. A 2019 study by BetterUp, published in Harvard Business Review, found that workplace belonging — the felt sense of being valued, included, and treated fairly — was linked to a 56% increase in job performance, a 50% reduction in turnover risk, and a 75% drop in employee sick days. For a 10,000-person company, the productivity gains alone topped fifty-two million dollars a year.1 The researchers call it belonging. The plain word employees use, when they describe a workplace where they actually feel that way, is almost always trust — trust that the rules apply to everyone, trust that the institution is paying attention, trust that their manager wants them to succeed. The same phenomenon, described in two vocabularies.

Trust does not arrive in a workplace by accident, and it does not arrive through a policy. Three things have to be working at once.

Three things hold up a workplace

Three conditions have to be present at the same time for a workplace to produce trust at any meaningful scale.

Structure — clear written policy, defined expectations, fair process applied consistently to everyone, including the boss.

Culture — the felt experience of working at the institution itself, separate from any individual manager: whether the place actually does what it says it does, whether complaints go anywhere, whether the values on the wall match what gets rewarded in practice.

Leadership — direct managers who know what they are doing, give honest and timely feedback, and actually want to be managing people.

None of these maps cleanly to a single role on the org chart. HR contributes to all three but cannot deliver any of them alone. Executives shape Culture most visibly but also set the tone for Structure and Leadership. Frontline managers carry Leadership most directly but model Culture in every interaction. The three are conditions, not job descriptions, and treating them as job descriptions is one of the ways organizations talk themselves out of fixing what is actually broken.

When all three are present, the workplace produces trust, and trust produces the outcomes the research keeps measuring — retention, engagement, lower absenteeism, fewer safety incidents, performance the spreadsheet can see. When any one of them fails, trust degrades, and the others start working overtime to compensate. Eventually they cannot.

Here is where most organizations make the same mistake. When trust is in trouble, the first move is almost always to fix Structure.

Why misdiagnosis is the real problem

The Edelman Trust Barometer's 2024 Special Report on trust at work makes the cost of misdiagnosing the problem concrete. Across the seven markets studied, when employees felt that executive management did not trust them, only 25% trusted their CEO, 43% trusted their direct manager, and 31% trusted their head of HR. When employees felt that executive management did trust them, the CEO trust number more than tripled, to 84%.2 Trust is reciprocal, and it is built by what leaders and managers actually do, in front of everyone, repeatedly, over time. It cannot be installed by a policy.

Zeynep Ton's research at MIT Sloan goes further still. Her work on what she calls the "good jobs strategy" — built across detailed studies of Costco, QuikTrip, Mercadona, and Trader Joe's — examines what happens when an employer gets the same three things working at once. QuikTrip's annual employee turnover sits at roughly one-fifth of the convenience store industry average, and its labour, space, and inventory productivity are significantly higher than that average.3 Mercadona, Spain's largest supermarket chain, runs at under 4% employee turnover.4 Costco pays well above the retail industry average, promotes its store managers from within, and roughly 60% of its employees have been with the company more than five years.5

The five operational tenets Ton identifies in these companies — investing in workers through pay and consistent schedules, simplifying workflow, giving employees voice in how to serve customers, cross-training, and staffing with enough slack that people are not rushing — are not all in one bucket. Pay structure, work design, and training systems are Structure. Worker voice and visible institutional respect are Culture. Slack staffing — giving managers the time and resources to actually lead instead of fighting fires — is Leadership.6 The reason these companies post the numbers they do is that they have built all three at once, and they protect each one against the others when budget pressure arrives. Their results are not the product of a single brilliant policy. They are the product of all three reinforcing one another.

Ton describes the alternative — what happens when companies treat their workforce as a cost line — as a vicious cycle. Low pay drives turnover, which drives poor execution, which drives low productivity, which drives poor service, which drives lower margins, which makes investing in people feel impossible, which keeps pay low.7 The cycle is not broken with a new attendance policy. It is broken by changing what the system rewards, which is a Culture problem and a Leadership problem before it is ever a Structure problem.

What this research keeps pointing at is the same uncomfortable conclusion. Trust is the output. Structure, Culture, and Leadership are the inputs. Hammering only on the input that is easiest to document does not rebuild the output.

Structure: necessary, not sufficient

Structure is what most HR people are trained to do well, and it is what most organizations notice when it is missing. It is the policy binder, the job description, the handbook, the performance template, the offer letter, the disciplinary procedure. It makes expectations legible to the person doing the job, and it makes accountability possible when those expectations are not met.

Marcus Buckingham's research at Gallup is direct about how much this matters. Of the twelve survey questions that consistently predict whether an employee is engaged at work, the very first one is whether they know what is expected of them.8 Without Structure, even motivated employees cannot succeed and even capable managers cannot manage. People do not stretch toward goals they cannot see, and they do not trust process that has never been written down.

Structure also matters for protection. In Canadian employment law, the bar for just-cause termination is high, and courts look for prior warnings, documented concerns, and progressive discipline applied consistently. Without Structure, an employer's defensible record is whatever the manager remembers, which in practice is not very much. A workforce that has watched policy be applied selectively does not trust that the next application will be any different. And employees who cannot trust the process to be fair are not employees who will raise problems early.

What Structure cannot do is bring the policy to life. It cannot make a manager believe in the feedback process. It cannot make an employee trust that the complaint procedure goes anywhere. It cannot make the handbook feel real to the person reading it on their first day. A policy without the other two is a document people work around, and in my experience it is the most common HR deliverable produced by organizations that are losing their best people one by one.

Culture: what the institution itself signals

Culture is the part most organizations name least clearly, and the one most likely to get rebranded into something safer. It is not benefits, though benefits matter. It is not employee experience as a survey score, though those scores measure something real. Culture is the felt institutional experience — what the place actually rewards, what it actually tolerates, and whether the gap between stated values and observed behaviour is small enough that people stop noticing it.

Deloitte's 2020 Human Capital Trends report frames the felt side of Culture as belonging, and identifies three building blocks: comfort (being treated fairly and respected), connection (meaningful relationships with colleagues and the organization's purpose), and contribution (understanding how one's work matters). The elements are described as additive — comfort enables connection, which enables contribution.9 The reverse is also true. When comfort goes — when the felt sense of fairness erodes — connection and contribution start eroding too, regardless of what the engagement survey says.

Daniel Coyle's work on high-performing teams in The Culture Code describes the same thing at a more granular scale. He calls them belonging cues — small signals that people are being seen as people, not processed as units. Eye contact, listening without interruption, remembering details from a previous conversation, acknowledging someone walking into a room. These are not soft skills. They are the daily, observable evidence that the institution itself notices the people inside it.10

Amy Edmondson's research at Harvard Business School pushes the point in a third direction. Psychological safety — the felt sense that one can speak up, ask questions, admit mistakes, and disagree without punishment — turns out to be the single most consistent predictor of how well a team learns and performs. Edmondson is clear that this state has to be actively built, especially by people in positions of authority, because the natural gravity of a hierarchy is silence.11 Psychological safety is to a team what Culture is to an organization. The same conditions, scaled up.

Culture fails first when something is wrong at the institutional level — when a complaint goes nowhere, when a policy applies to some employees but not others, when a senior leader behaves in a way that would have ended the career of anyone three rungs lower, when a layoff is handled in a way that tells the survivors to update their resumes. None of these failures shows up on the org chart. All of them register in the floor's nervous system within about a week, and the institution's stated commitments stop being believable for everyone who saw what happened.

This is also where executive leadership shows up most visibly, whether it intends to or not. Peter Aceto, the former CEO of Tangerine, frames the executive job in his book Weology as putting the collective ahead of personal status — sitting among the team rather than above it, being transparent about hard decisions, taking the customer call yourself.12 The argument is not that executives have to be saintly. The argument is that the culture of an organization is downstream of what the most senior people in it visibly do, and trying to fix Culture from any other position is hard work for limited return.

Leadership: do the managers actually want to manage?

Leadership is the part where the previous two either pay off or collapse. The handbook can be perfect and the benefits can be generous. If a person's direct manager does not give them feedback, does not know their goals, and visibly does not want to be managing other people, none of the rest of it matters very much.

Gallup's long-running research on engagement places approximately 70% of the variance in employee engagement at the feet of the direct manager.13 Buckingham's work points at a related problem: organizations consistently promote their best individual contributor into management without checking whether that person actually wants to lead other people. The result is that the company loses two good employees at once — a great worker becomes an unhappy and disengaged manager, and the team they now lead gets the worst version of both.14 Laszlo Bock's analysis at Google, in Work Rules!, reaches a similar conclusion from a different angle: hiring rigorously and managing the two tails of performance — top performers and bottom — is where the highest-leverage management work happens, not in the middle.15

The retail leadership writer Kit Campoy has made a career of describing what happens when the system stops asking whether managers want the job. Her observation, repeated in different forms across her writing, is that when a frontline manager is skilled enough to absorb impossible demands without complaint — covering shifts, swallowing the schedule, taking the work home unpaid — the company never has to confront what it is actually asking. The store hits its numbers. The strategy looks like it is working. It is not working. It is breaking the manager, slowly, in a way no spreadsheet picks up.16 Good managers can mask system failures for years. Eventually they leave too, and the gap between what the organization thought it had and what was actually holding the place together becomes obvious all at once.

This is also the part of the workplace that explains why HR people, often to the frustration of the employee asking the question, keep redirecting things back to the manager. When an HR representative asks "have you spoken to your manager about this?", they are not blowing the employee off and they are not telling them they do not care. They are pointing back to where the relationship that actually matters most is supposed to be formed. Most of what employees mean by "trust" is built or broken in their direct manager interactions, week after week, because that is where the bulk of their working life happens. HR cannot substitute for that relationship and should not try to. What HR can do is make sure the relationship is possible — by hiring managers who want to manage, training them, holding them accountable for how they treat people, and noticing when one of them is failing the team.

The same logic explains why HR will push back when a manager tries to hand off a hard conversation. HR can prepare a manager for a difficult discussion, sit in on it when warranted, and debrief afterward — but the conversation itself belongs to the manager. The reason is not procedural. It is that the manager's authority and trust are what is being established or eroded in that conversation. A manager who never delivers the difficult message themselves is one whose team learns to see them as ornamental, with the real work being done by someone else. Competence and trustworthiness are earned in those moments, and outsourcing them to HR forfeits both. Most managers, asked plainly, would rather be the person their team trusts than the person their team works around.

The hardest question I have learned to ask of a struggling team is also the simplest: do the managers actually want to manage? It is not rhetorical. People get promoted because they were the best at the work, not because anyone asked whether they wanted to lead others. They are not the villains of the story. They are people who were given the wrong job, often without being asked, and the cost of that mismatch shows up everywhere except the org chart.

The HR job: diagnose before prescribing

Most of the work of a good HR partner — the part that is harder to teach and easier to skip — is figuring out which of the three is actually broken before reaching for a tool. When something goes wrong at work and someone calls HR, the easiest move is to ask whether the policy is clean. The harder move, and almost always the more useful one, is to ask whether the policy is the right thing to be looking at.

A wave of resignations on a single team is rarely a Structure problem. It is usually a Leadership problem the structure failed to surface in time. A complaint that gets escalated to senior leadership because nobody acted on it the first time is not a Structure problem either. It is a Culture problem, and the fix is whether anyone actually does something this time. A team where the same manager has been underperforming for three years and nothing has been done is not a Structure problem. It is a Leadership problem the institution has been protecting itself from looking at.

These diagnoses take longer than writing a policy. They produce less satisfying-looking deliverables. They name people, not procedures. And most organizations actively resist them, because the Structure response is the visible, defensible, billable one. Writing a policy looks like action. Telling a leadership team that several of their middle managers do not actually want to manage people, and that no policy will fix that, looks like trouble.

The hard part of HR is being willing to look like trouble when looking like trouble is the right answer.

The framework is simple. The practice is not.

Three things have to be true at once for trust to exist, and trust is what produces the outcomes everyone says they want. As a framework, that is not complicated. The challenge comes in the practice — making sure the right person is guiding the hard conversation, promoting only the managers who actually want to lead, and pausing long enough to ask which of the three has broken before reaching for the disciplinary form.

Ashley Herd's framework for managers in The Manager Method — pause, consider, act — is built around exactly this kind of restraint. Pause long enough to stop reacting at the speed of email. Consider who is affected, what is actually known, what is being assumed, and what the human ripple effects of the decision will be. Then act deliberately, with the response that fits the problem.17 Most of what is described in this article is a version of that discipline, applied to the level above any single decision. Pause before assuming the policy is the answer. Consider which of Structure, Culture, or Leadership is actually broken. Then act.

The research community uses the word belonging because it is measurable — through surveys, retention data, performance correlations, and dollar figures calculated across thousands of employees. All of that is useful, and the numbers are there for anyone who needs the business case. What employees themselves describe, when they are asked plainly about a workplace where they thrived, is something simpler. They trusted the rules. They trusted the institution. They trusted their manager. When all three were true at the same time, the work was good and they stayed. When any one of them broke, they started counting the months until they could leave.

That is what trust is made of, and trust is what builds workplaces that work.
