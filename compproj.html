The Bear’s Den: Compensation structure and pay equity review

A capstone-style compensation project based on ADP’s Entry‑Level Compensation and Benefits Analyst certificate, built around a fictional non‑profit recreation facility in Alberta’s Rocky Mountains.

Context

The Bear’s Den is a fictional non‑profit recreation facility serving a small town in Alberta’s Rocky Mountains. As a new Compensation Analyst, I was asked to review the organization’s pay practices for competitiveness, internal equity, and alignment with a sustainable non‑profit budget.

I used a synthetic employee dataset of hourly and salaried roles (front desk, concessions, aquatics, fitness, maintenance, housekeeping, administration, and leadership) to design a full compensation review that mirrors real‑world practice.

Project goals

  • Define a clear compensation philosophy tailored to a small, mission‑driven non‑profit.
  • Evaluate jobs and build a grade‑based salary structure tied to external market data.
  • Use Excel to calculate key compensation metrics and identify compression and equity issues.
  • Develop budgeted recommendations and a board‑ready narrative for implementation.

This project is intentionally Excel‑heavy: the workbook shows the underlying math, while the board report focuses on plain‑language findings and recommendations.

Assignment structure

The project is organized like a capstone assignment for a junior compensation analyst. The core deliverable is an Excel model, supported by a written report to the Board and optional presentation slides.

1. Define compensation philosophy & strategy

  • Draft a compensation philosophy that balances mission, market competitiveness, and fiscal responsibility.
  • Set target market positioning (e.g., 50th percentile for support roles, 60th for safety‑critical roles).
  • Outline governance: who approves structures, exceptions, and periodic reviews.

2. Job analysis & evaluation

  • Group roles into job families (Guest Services, Aquatics, Fitness, Maintenance, Housekeeping, Concessions, Admin, Leadership).
  • Use a point‑factor method with defined compensable factors (knowledge, problem solving, accountability, working conditions).
  • Score a representative set of jobs and translate total points into a job hierarchy.

3. Market benchmarking

  • Research Alberta recreation and non‑profit market data for key roles (e.g., lifeguards, recreation supervisors, managers).
  • Select peer sources and define how to use P25 / P50 / P75 data for Bear’s Den.
  • Build a market data tab in Excel and link benchmark values into the pay structure.

4. Build grade structure & pay ranges

  • Create 8–10 salary grades with midpoint progressions and range spreads by level.
  • Calculate minimum, midpoint, and maximum for each grade using consistent formulas.
  • Assign jobs to grades based on evaluation and market data.

5. Calculate compensation metrics

  • Convert hourly rates to annualized pay where needed and attach each employee to a grade.
  • Compute compa‑ratio, range penetration, and market ratio for each employee.
  • Identify pay structure outliers (below minimum, above maximum) and instances of pay compression.
  • Slice results by department, tenure, and demographic fields to surface equity issues.

6. Budget & implementation planning

  • Model the cost of bringing all employees to at least range minimum.
  • Estimate additional investment for key market and compression adjustments.
  • Propose phased implementation options aligned with a non‑profit budget.

7. Board‑level communication

  • Summarize current‑state risks and opportunities in a concise executive summary.
  • Translate Excel findings into clear charts and tables suitable for a Board package.
  • Outline recommendations, risk trade‑offs, and a maintenance plan for the structure.

Excel model: key components

The workbook is designed to be auditable and reusable. Each tab has a clear purpose, and complex calculations are broken out into helper columns so another analyst or leader can follow the logic.

Core tabs
10

Instructions, employee master, job inventory, market data, job evaluation, pay structure, metrics dashboard, outliers, budget modelling, and visuals.

Key formulas

Compa‑ratio, range penetration, market ratio, salary differentials, red/green circle flags, and summary KPIs by department and job family.

Visuals

Grade structures, compa‑ratio distributions, equity views, and budget impact charts designed for direct export into a Board report.

Examples of analysis built in Excel

  • Conditional formatting to highlight employees below minimum or above maximum.
  • Department‑level dashboards showing average compa‑ratio and market ratio.
  • Comparisons of supervisor pay vs direct reports to spot compression.
  • Simple equity checks by sex within grades using summary tables and visuals.

If you open the workbook, you can trace each recommendation in the Board report back to a specific tab, calculation, or visualization in Excel.
