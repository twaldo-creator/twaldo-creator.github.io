Wide‑ranging HR and People & Culture experience from the field

Most of my time was spent helping leaders sort out people problems in active stores: performance, behaviour, leaves, and “how do we handle this?” situations. While I was there, I also listened to the team, closed the loop on feedback, and looked for small, practical changes that made work feel a bit better.

Employee relations & investigations

I handled sensitive cases in a way that was fair, well‑documented, and defensible, while still trying to keep trust with employees and leaders where I could.

Examples included harassment and conduct complaints, safety violations, falsified records, and accommodation disputes; I coached leaders on documentation, progressive discipline, and performance management, pushed back on weak or premature terminations, and helped build stronger, more defensible cases when a termination was the right next step.

Leaves, accommodations & return to work

I managed complex disability and leave files with leaders, third‑party administrators, and Legal, aiming for solutions that met our obligations and still felt humane to the people involved.

I carried 20–30 leave and accommodation files at a time, explained ideas like undue hardship and frustration of contract in plain language, and partnered on staged return‑to‑work plans that the employee, the team, and the store could actually run.

Engagement & culture

I used engagement data, in‑store conversations, and benefits and recognition programs to help leaders improve the day‑to‑day experience for frontline teams.

Before visits, I reviewed engagement results and comments, then used them to shape 1:1 and small‑group conversations in the store; I highlighted benefits and programs in ways that made sense to hourly staff and helped leaders pick a small number of visible actions that showed they were listening and following through.

Policy interpretation & practical compliance

I closed gaps between written policy and what was actually happening in stores, and explained rules in plain language so leaders and frontline teams knew what was expected.

This included spotting and correcting a nationwide misapplication of a leave‑of‑absence policy (60 days used in practice vs. a written six‑month entitlement) and creating simple tools, like a one‑page co‑op tracking form, so compliance felt doable instead of bureaucratic.

How I work

How I approach HR and People & Culture problems

I start with what’s happening in the store right now, then pull in the policies, laws, past cases, and data that actually matter for that situation. The cases below show how I think through real people and culture problems instead of jumping straight to a standard answer.

Case Example
Spotting a co‑op gap and fixing it

On a store visit, a manager introduced two co‑op students. I realised I hadn’t seen any paperwork for them, even though there was supposed to be a standard process, so I asked the manager to complete the forms right away so the students were properly covered.

After that visit, I sent a short check‑in to all stores asking who had co‑op students and whether documents were on file. Once we confirmed multiple placements with missing paperwork in a single semester, I set up a simple Microsoft Forms/Excel tracker owned by HR and walked District leaders through the new process and the risk; that gave us visibility and better compliance without adding a heavy new system.

Case Example
Handling falsified safety records in a retail store

During a store visit, a team member told me their signature was being forged on joint health and safety committee minutes. I thanked them for speaking up, explained what I could and couldn’t share about any investigation, and asked if they were comfortable being contacted again so we could follow up properly.

From there, I quietly reviewed the safety binder, looked for patterns, and identified who should have been on the committee but might not be attending; I then looped in the District leader and investigations team so the case was handled formally. My focus was on protecting the reporter, getting clear facts, and recommending a response that treated the behaviour as a serious integrity issue, not just a paperwork mistake.

Case Example
Resetting attendance expectations in an early‑morning team

Before I moved into HR, I took over a store where the early‑morning team was arriving anywhere from 5 to 45 minutes late, and the prior manager had not held anyone accountable. There was no specific tardiness policy, only a general expectation that staff arrived on time and that managers monitored timecards, which gave me room to design a fair approach.

I made a plan to address tardiness with the team that was fair and consistent, applied it to everyone on the shift, and followed through on the conversations and documentation each week; it led to a clear drop in late arrivals and made it easier for the opening manager to plan work. Later, as an HR partner, this became the kind of structured approach I coached Store Managers to use in their own teams.

Case Example
Performance issues in a busy store

A Store Manager wanted to terminate an underperforming team member quickly, but there was little documentation and only broad complaints. Moving straight to termination would have created risk and damaged trust with the rest of the team.

I helped the leader slow down long enough to clarify expectations, gather specific recent examples, and set a realistic improvement plan where it made sense; we built a fair, well‑documented process that gave the employee a real chance to improve and gave the company a defensible file if termination later became necessary.

Case Example
Low engagement and low trust in leadership

Engagement survey results flagged low trust in leadership and low scores on communication in a high‑volume store. Leaders were worried about turnover and morale but weren’t sure where to start without making big promises they couldn’t keep.

I reviewed survey comments, then visited the store to walk the floor with the leader and speak with every on‑shift team member I could; I used what I heard to help the leader choose two or three visible, realistic changes—better huddles, clearer follow‑through on issues, and targeted recognition—and we set a check‑in to review impact. The focus was on rebuilding trust through consistent actions, not launching a big new program.

Case Example
Clearing long‑term leave and LTD backlogs

Over time, a backlog of long‑term medical leave and LTD files had built up. Some team members had been off for years with no recent documentation or clear plan to return, and leaders weren’t sure what, if anything, they could do next.

I took my share of these older files end to end: contacting employees, requesting updated medical information where appropriate, and either supporting a return to work or partnering with Legal on possible frustration of contract and final entitlements; the goal was to move each file to a clear, defensible outcome instead of leaving it in limbo.
