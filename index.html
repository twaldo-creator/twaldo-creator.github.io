How I work

How I approach HR and People & Culture problems

I start by understanding what is really happening on the ground, then layer in policy, legislation, internal precedents, engagement data, and practical constraints before recommending a path forward. These examples show how I think through different kinds of people and culture problems.

Case Example
Spotting a co‑op gap and fixing it

On a store visit, a manager introduced two co‑op students. I knew I hadn’t seen any paperwork for them, even though there was supposed to be a standard process. I asked the manager to complete the forms right away so the students were properly covered.

After that visit, I sent a short survey to all stores asking who had co‑op students and whether documents were on file. We found several placements with missing paperwork in just one semester. I set up a simple online form that fed into a spreadsheet owned by HR so managers could register new students in a few minutes and walked District leaders through the new process and the risk. That gave us visibility and better compliance without adding a heavy new system.

Case Example
Handling falsified safety records in a retail store

During a store visit, a team member told me their signature was being forged on joint health and safety committee minutes. I thanked them for speaking up, explained what I could and couldn’t share about any investigation, and asked if they were comfortable being contacted again. From there, I gathered basic details about what they had seen and which documents were involved.

In this kind of situation, I quietly review the safety binder, look for patterns, and identify who should be on the committee but might not be attending. I then loop in the District leader and investigations team so the case is handled formally. Falsifying safety records is a serious breach of trust, even if no incident has occurred yet, so my focus is on protecting the reporter, getting clear facts, and recommending a response that treats the behaviour as a serious integrity issue.

Case Example
Coaching managers through availability and scheduling limits

In many stores, managers gradually agree to more and more limited availability for staff. Over time, the schedule becomes hard to cover, and the remaining team members burn out. Leaders then ask HR to “fix” the problem, even though the issue came from decisions made in the store.

I help leaders separate true restrictions (for example, medical or family needs) from preferences, and coach managers on how to have honest, respectful conversations about role requirements. HR’s role is to ensure those discussions respect policy and human rights obligations while keeping psychological safety and fairness at the centre. When adjustments are needed, I also watch for constructive dismissal risk so changes to hours or status are handled in a clear and defensible way.

Case Example
Performance issues in a busy store

A Store Manager wants to terminate an underperforming team member quickly, but there is little documentation and only broad complaints. Moving straight to termination would create risk and damage trust.

I help the leader slow down enough to do this properly: clarify expectations, gather specific examples, and set a realistic improvement plan where appropriate. We build a fair, well‑documented process that gives the employee a chance to improve and gives the company a defensible file if termination becomes necessary.

Case Example
Low engagement and low trust in leadership

Engagement survey results flag low trust in leadership and low scores on communication in a high‑volume store. Leaders are worried about turnover and morale, but aren’t sure where to start without making big promises they can’t keep.

I review survey comments, then visit the store to walk the floor with the leader and speak with every on‑shift team member. I use what I hear to help the leader choose two or three visible, realistic changes—like better huddles, clear follow‑through on issues, and targeted recognition—and we set a check‑in to review impact. The focus is on rebuilding trust through consistent actions, not grand programs.
