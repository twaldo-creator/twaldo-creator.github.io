How I work

How I approach HR and People & Culture problems

1. Performance issues in a busy store

Situation: A Store Manager wants to terminate an underperforming team member quickly.

Questions & information: What has already been documented? What specific examples can the leader give? What support or expectations were clearly communicated? How is this affecting the team and customers?

Policy & framework: Performance management policies, progressive discipline guidelines, and any relevant past precedents.

Advisory stance: I help the leader slow down enough to do this properly: clarify expectations, set a realistic improvement plan where appropriate, and document fairly. If termination is the right outcome, we get there with defensible, humane steps.

2. Complex accommodation or leave case

Situation: A leader is frustrated by a long‑running medical leave and wants to know “if we can just end this.”

Questions & information: What medical information do we have? What accommodations have been tried? What is the impact on the team and scheduling? Has the employee been kept in the loop?

Policy & framework: Human rights obligations, undue hardship, internal LOA policies, and case law/precedents.

Advisory stance: I explain the legal and human context in plain language, outline options (e.g., modified duties, gradual RTW, frustration of contract), and help the leader choose a path that balances compliance, fairness, and operational reality.

3. Policy–practice gap creating risk

Situation: Store practices have drifted away from written policy in a way that creates risk (e.g., LOA caps, undocumented co‑op placements).

Questions & information: What is actually happening in stores? Why did people start doing it this way? What do leaders and team members think the rule is?

Policy & framework: Written policy, collective agreement language (where applicable), and legal obligations.

Advisory stance: I identify the real‑world gap, confirm the correct interpretation, and then co‑design simple tools, training, and communications that make it easy for leaders and teams to get back into alignment.

4. Low engagement and low trust in leadership

Situation: Engagement survey results flag low trust in leadership and low scores on communication in a high‑volume store.

Questions & information: What specific feedback did employees share in comments? What have leaders already tried? How do team members describe day‑to‑day interactions with their manager?

Policy & framework: Engagement survey themes, values statements, any existing recognition or communication expectations.

Advisory stance: I use on‑site HR visits and small‑group conversations to hear more detail, then help the leader choose two or three simple, visible changes (e.g., regular huddles, better follow‑up on issues, benefit reminders) and set a check‑in to review impact. The focus is on rebuilding trust through consistent actions, not grand programs.
