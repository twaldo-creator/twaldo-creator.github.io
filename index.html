Core Skills

Wide‑ranging HR and People & Culture experience from the field

Most of my time was spent helping leaders sort out people problems in active stores: performance, behaviour, leaves, and “how do we handle this?” situations. While I was there, I also listened to the team, closed the loop on feedback, and looked for small, practical changes that made work feel a bit better.

Employee relations & investigations

I handled sensitive cases in a way that was fair, well‑documented, and defensible, while still trying to keep trust with employees and leaders where I could. Examples included harassment and conduct complaints, safety violations, falsified records, and accommodation disputes; I coached leaders on documentation, progressive discipline, and performance management, pushed back on weak or premature terminations, and helped build stronger cases when a termination was the right next step.

Leaves, accommodations & return to work

I managed complex disability and leave files with leaders, third‑party administrators, and Legal, aiming for solutions that met our obligations and still felt humane to the people involved. I usually carried 20–30 leave and accommodation files at a time, explained ideas like undue hardship and frustration of contract in plain language, and worked on return‑to‑work plans that the employee, team, and store could actually run.

Engagement & culture

I used engagement data, in‑store conversations, and benefits and recognition programs to help leaders improve the day‑to‑day experience for frontline teams. Before visits, I reviewed engagement results and comments, then used them to shape 1:1 and small‑group conversations; I highlighted benefits and programs in ways that made sense to hourly staff and helped leaders pick a small number of visible actions that showed they were listening.

Policy interpretation & practical compliance

I closed gaps between written policy and what was actually happening in stores and explained rules in plain language so leaders and frontline teams knew what was expected. That included spotting and correcting a misapplication of a leave‑of‑absence policy (60 days used in practice vs. a written six‑month entitlement) and creating simple tools, like a one‑page co‑op tracking form, so compliance felt doable instead of bureaucratic.

Frontline leadership & change

Before moving into Field HR, I spent more than ten years in store leadership, and that experience shaped how I supported leaders later. I ran high‑volume stores with up to 50 employees and about $7.5M in annual sales and designed 1‑ to 4‑week training programs for new and promoted leaders, which helped me give advice that fit how busy stores actually run.

Compensation, data & evidence‑based HR

I worked on basic compensation and benefits questions, HRIS cleanup, and simple people data so HR decisions relied less on anecdotes. I cleaned up more than 300 HRIS address exceptions in two weeks so T4s could go out on time for roughly 3,000 employees and then helped adjust the onboarding steps that were causing the errors; I also completed an entry‑level compensation and benefits certificate and began data analytics training to support more evidence‑based HR work.

How I work

How I approach HR and People & Culture problems

I usually started with what was actually happening on the ground, then worked outward to the policy, data, and level of risk that mattered. These examples show how I handled real employee relations, compliance, and operations issues without defaulting to a one size fits all answer.

Case Example
Spotting a Co-op gap and fixing it

On a store visit, a manager introduced me to Co-op students I had never seen paperwork for, which meant they might be in the building without proper waivers or insurance. I fixed the immediate gap with that store, then checked all locations and found about fifteen undocumented placements in one semester.

I confirmed requirements with Legal, then built a simple Microsoft Forms and Excel tracker so managers could log each Co-op placement and paperwork status. Rolling it out through district leaders made it part of normal planning, gave HR visibility, and gave leaders a clear, easy way to stay compliant.

Case Example
Handling falsified safety records in a store

During a store visit, a team member quietly told me their name was appearing on safety committee minutes for meetings they had not attended. I thanked them, set expectations about confidentiality and outcomes, and asked straightforward questions to understand what they had seen.

I then reviewed the safety binder and recent minutes, comparing names and attendance to see if this was a one off or a pattern of falsified records. I escalated my findings to the appropriate leader and investigations partners and helped reinforce expectations with other stores so safety work reflected reality, not just paperwork.

Case Example
Resetting attendance expectations in an early morning team

As a store manager, I inherited an early morning freight team of about fifteen people, with roughly six regularly late for shift start. There was only a general expectation to be on time, no clear structure.

I reviewed our attendance guidance, met with the team, and explained why start times mattered for truck unloads and opening. I set up a simple approach to track lateness, coach on patterns, and move to written conversations if needed. By following through each week, lateness dropped sharply within a month.

Case Example
Supporting a manager through a complex performance plan

A senior leader instructed a store manager to put an assistant manager on a performance plan, but the first draft was too vague to support either real improvement or a defensible termination. I reviewed several versions, pushed for specific examples and expectations, and kept asking questions about past accountability and support.

When it was clear the manager needed more help, I built a short deck and coached him through what a strong plan needed to include. With clearer expectations and support, the assistant manager improved and successfully completed the plan.

Case Example
Fixing HRIS address errors before T4s were due

Right before T4s were due, payroll flagged more than 300 address errors that were blocking uploads for roughly three thousand employees. I worked through the backlog in about two weeks, correcting records so T4 processing could proceed on time.

While doing that, I found root causes: postal codes were not required in the applicant system, and stores were not always completing onboarding. I partnered with HRIS to improve how address data was captured and to create a weekly onboarding status report so issues could be caught earlier.

Case Example
Clearing long term leave and LTD backlogs

A backlog of long term medical leave and disability files had built up over several years, leaving employees, leaders, and HR without clear next steps. My share of the backlog involved reviewing each file, re establishing contact where possible, and requesting updated medical information.

From there, I worked with leaders and Legal to assess whether a return, accommodation, resignation, or frustration of contract made sense. Clearing those files reduced uncertainty, gave leaders staffing clarity, and brought long running cases to clear, documented endpoints.
