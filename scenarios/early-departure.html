The Early Departure: When a Practice Becomes a Policy

A new hire pulls you aside during training to tell you that the senior field team leaves job sites early and claims full days. Here's how I'd respond — and why the length of time it's been happening changes everything.

The situation

A new employee who is currently in training approaches you with a concern. While working with an experienced field team, they were told that it's common practice to leave job sites early and not return to the office — while still being paid for a full day. A senior team member directly told the trainee to "just go home" at the end of the job rather than check in or pick up additional work.

The employee feels uncomfortable and unsure whether this is acceptable practice or something they should be concerned about.

In the moment

Same starting point as any concern raised by someone new: let them know they did the right thing by coming forward, and clarify the actual policy so they have the correct information going forward. They shouldn't be left wondering whether they're the one who misunderstood something.

I'd also think carefully about confidentiality. If this practice is widespread and the trainee is seen as the person who "ruined a good thing," that's a real risk. It's not a reason to avoid investigating, but it does shape how I'd protect them throughout the process.

Before interviewing anyone: gather the hard evidence

This one is considerably more serious than a conduct complaint — there's potential financial loss to the organization, and depending on what we find, termination may be on the table. That changes the sequencing. Before I sit down with anyone from the field team, I want as complete a picture as possible.

That means pulling dispatch records, sign-in logs, and GPS tracking data if it's available. The goal is to go into those interviews with facts, not just an allegation. It also means I'd brief senior leadership early, so they're not surprised if the investigation leads somewhere significant.

Reviewing the policy picture first

Before conducting any interviews, I'd check the existing policy on attendance and timekeeping — whether that's in the employee handbook, a standalone policy, or a collective agreement if one applies. Critically: when did each of the employees in question last acknowledge that policy? That matters for what's defensible on our end.

The interviews

I'd start broad. "Walk me through last Thursday's shift — what was the job, who was there, did you run into any challenges?" Once the basics are established, I'd narrow: "What time did you leave the site? Have you ever been directed to leave a job early? How did you respond? Do you remember signing the timekeeping policy?"

Tolerated practices become de facto policies. If this has been going on for a long time without correction, we're in a weaker position to discipline — and we need to be honest about that.

When assessing outcomes, the relevant factors are: how often this occurred, how significant the early departures were, who was directing it, whether employees were aware of the timekeeping policy at the time, and whether they could reasonably have known the behaviour wasn't approved. Those factors will determine what's appropriate — and what's defensible.

If it's broader than one team

If this has been a widespread, unaddressed practice, we'd be on weaker footing to discipline aggressively. That's uncomfortable, but it's accurate. The right move in that case is to either launch an updated attendance policy with fresh sign-offs, or get sign-offs on the existing one — and use that as a clear reset point. Expectations get re-set with supervisors and managers on accurate timekeeping and what to do when a job finishes early.

Going forward, the tools matter too. If GPS tracking or job-completion logging isn't in place yet, this is the moment to make the case for it — not as surveillance, but as a system that protects both the organization and employees who are doing the right thing.
