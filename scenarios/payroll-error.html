Operations · Employee Trust Scenario 03

The Payroll Error: Own It Fast, Fix It Faster

A routine audit surfaces overtime underpayments going back three months. No one meant for it to happen. That doesn't change what you owe people — or how quickly you need to move.

The situation

During a routine audit, you discover that several employees have been underpaid overtime for the past three months due to a system configuration error in the HRIS/payroll system.

Immediate response

The first call is to leadership. Not to report a crisis, but to flag that there's a problem and that we're already working on it. Nobody wants to hear about a payroll error for the first time from an employee who noticed their cheque was short.

From there, I'd calculate the full scope of the shortages — every affected employee, every affected pay period — and confirm realistic timelines for reissuance. If the amounts are meaningful and payday is more than a week out, an off-cycle payment is worth pursuing. People earn their overtime; they shouldn't have to wait an extra cycle to get it.

Simultaneously: identify the configuration error, correct it, and audit at least two subsequent pay cycles to confirm it's actually fixed and not still running in the background.

How to communicate it

Own it fully. Don't bury the lede, don't hedge, and don't make employees feel like they're finding out by accident.

Communication goes out in two stages. First to managers, so they're prepared to answer questions from their teams — not blindsided when someone walks up to them at the start of a shift. Then directly to employees, with a memo and an FAQ that covers the basics clearly: what happened, how it was caught, what's been corrected, and exactly when the missing funds will arrive.

The tone matters as much as the content. A genuine apology, a clear explanation, and a specific date. Not "as soon as possible" — an actual date. Employees will forgive an honest mistake. They're less forgiving of vague non-answers.

Preventing it from happening again

Two questions worth asking after the immediate fix is done: who owns payroll accuracy in this organization, and is that ownership clear to everyone involved? Errors like this often happen in the gap between HR, payroll, and IT — where each team assumes someone else is validating the output.

The second question is whether the audit cadence is right. If this error ran for three months before being caught in a "routine" audit, that's worth examining. More frequent spot-checks on overtime calculations specifically — especially after any system changes or updates — would catch these earlier. The goal isn't to create bureaucracy; it's to make sure that when the system does something unexpected, someone notices within days, not quarters.
